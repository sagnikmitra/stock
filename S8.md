## Purpose of This Session

This session is designed to teach students how to read the market **before taking a swing trade** and how to combine that market context with a structured daily-chart strategy.

The instructor’s central idea is simple:

> A good swing trade is not only about a chart pattern.  
> It must be supported by broader market context, liquidity, volatility behavior, and institutional flow.

So Session 8 adds a **market-preparation layer** before trade execution.

# 2. Pre-Market Context Reading

A major section of Session 8 is about understanding the market **before the 9:15 AM open**.

The instructor explains that many traders fail because they jump directly to stock charts without first reading the broader environment.

## 2.1 GIFT Nifty as the first reference

The instructor explains that:

- **GIFT Nifty** is the offshore reference for Indian market sentiment
- It has replaced **SGX Nifty**
- Students are shown how to locate it on TradingView

### Important clarification
Older traders often still say “SGX Nifty,” but that reference is outdated.  
The relevant offshore reference now is:

- **GIFT Nifty**
- Located in **GIFT City, Gujarat**

### Practical implication
Before the Indian market opens, GIFT Nifty gives a clue about likely opening sentiment:
- If GIFT Nifty is strong, the opening bias may be bullish
- If GIFT Nifty is weak, the opening bias may be bearish

This is not treated as a perfect predictor, but as an important directional clue.

---

## 2.2 Dow Jones and E-mini Dow Futures

The second major global cue is the US market.

The instructor specifically discusses:
- **Dow Jones Industrial Average**
- **E-mini Dow Futures**
- TradingView ticker: `YM1!`

### Core rule taught
There is a **direct correlation** between:
- **GIFT Nifty**
- **E-mini Dow Futures**

### Meaning
If Dow Futures are green overnight:
- It often supports bullish sentiment in Nifty

If Dow Futures are weak:
- It often weakens the probability of a strong bullish Indian open

This is not presented as absolute certainty.  
It is presented as **context alignment**.

---

## 2.3 Gold and Crude Oil as risk backdrop indicators

The third major set of pre-market cues:
- **Gold**
- **Crude Oil**

### Correlation rule taught
The instructor explains an **inverse / negative correlation** relationship between:
- **Gold / Crude**
- **Dow Futures / broader risk sentiment**

### Interpretation
If:
- Gold is rising sharply
- Crude is rising sharply

That may indicate:
- Risk-off sentiment
- Uncertainty
- Pressure on equities

If gold and crude are calm or falling while equity futures are strong, it may support a more bullish equity environment.

### Why this matters
The point is not to trade gold or oil directly.  
The point is to understand the **background emotional state of the market**.

---

## 2.4 The full pre-market checklist

The instructor repeatedly pushes students to check the following before trading:

1. **GIFT Nifty**
2. **Dow Jones**
3. **Dow Futures (`YM1!`)**
4. **Gold**
5. **Crude Oil**

### Practical decision rule
- If these signals broadly align bullish → bullish trades have better context
- If these signals are conflicting → trade with more caution or stand aside
- If these signals are clearly hostile → avoid forcing long swing setups

### Main lesson
Do not start with the stock.  
Start with the market environment.

# 3. Volatility and Liquidity

The session then moves into foundational trading concepts.

---

## 3.1 Volatility

The instructor explains volatility in simple, practical language.

### Definition used
Volatility is associated with:
- Tension
- Anxiety
- Uncertainty
- Unpredictability in near-term movement

### Trading interpretation
A market is volatile when:
- The next move is not stable
- Candles become erratic
- Noise increases
- Stop-losses can get hit easily

### Important nuance
The instructor does **not** frame volatility as inherently bad.

Instead:
- Volatility creates opportunity
- But uncontrolled volatility creates bad trade quality

So traders need **usable volatility**, not chaos.

---

## 3.2 Liquidity

Liquidity is explained as the ability to:
- Enter a trade smoothly
- Exit a trade smoothly
- Avoid large slippage

### Why liquidity matters in swing trading
A stock may look technically attractive, but if liquidity is poor:
- Stop-loss execution may fail
- Price may move too sharply on low participation
- Trade quality deteriorates

### Instructor emphasis
Swing traders need:
- Sufficient participation
- Reliable volume
- Smooth execution

The instructor connects this back to scanner logic from previous sessions.

---

## 3.3 Scanner volume filter as liquidity filter

The instructor recalls the prior volume filter:
- `volume ≥ 1.0` relative volume threshold

### Important interpretation
This filter is not just about momentum.

It is primarily about:
- Tradability
- Liquidity quality
- Avoiding dead stocks

This is an important teaching point because beginners often misunderstand volume as only “speed” or “momentum,” whereas here it is being used as a **minimum quality control layer**.

---

## 3.4 Example reference — Adani Energy

The session references **Adani Energy** around a **52-week high**.

### Purpose of the example
The example is used to show that:
- Even a strong uptrend is not enough on its own

### Core takeaway
A strong chart is necessary, but not sufficient.

# 4. FII / FPI Flow as Confluence

The next layer added to the strategy framework is institutional participation.

The instructor explains that FIIs have an outsized influence on market direction.

---

## 4.1 Terminology

- **FII** = Foreign Institutional Investor
- Updated official terminology: **FPI** = Foreign Portfolio Investor

The instructor notes that people still commonly say FII, even though FPI is the updated formal usage.

---

## 4.2 Why FIIs / FPIs matter

These participants influence:
- Nifty direction
- Broader market sentiment
- Equity flows at scale

Because of their size, their buying or selling can support or oppose chart-based trades.

---

## 4.3 The three daily institutional numbers to track

The instructor says students should watch three categories:

1. **Net buying / selling in cash equities**
2. **Net position in index futures**
3. **Net position in index options**

These numbers act as confirmation layers.

---

## 4.4 Core rule for trade confirmation

If FIIs are:
- **Net buyers in cash equities**  
then bullish swing setups get stronger confirmation.

If FIIs are:
- **Net sellers**, especially while a chart appears bullish  
then confidence should be reduced.

### Key discipline rule
Do **not** trade only because FIIs are buying.

Instead:
- Trade the chart
- Use FII flow as backup confirmation

This is one of the cleaner points in the session:
> Institutional flow is confluence, not the main trigger.

# 5. ABC Strategy

This is the main tactical strategy block introduced in Session 8.

The instructor introduces a new swing setup built on three aligned conditions.

---

## 5.1 Full form of ABC

- **A = 50-day Simple Moving Average**
- **B = Bollinger Band**
- **C = Green Candle**

This is a daily timeframe swing strategy.

---

## 5.2 Strategy logic

The setup is based on a stock that is:
- In an overall recoverable / bullish structure
- Testing weakness
- Then showing a valid bullish re-entry signal

The strategy tries to capture:
- Reversion from temporary weakness
- Reclaim of structure
- Confirmation through candle behavior

---

## 5.3 Condition A — 50 SMA

The 50-day SMA is used as a trend filter and structural guide.

### Interpretation
A stock is stronger when:
- Price is above the 50 SMA

A recovery / reversal setup may also be valid when:
- Price reclaims the 50 SMA from below

### Why 50 SMA matters here
It acts as:
- Trend context
- Entry confirmation support
- Trailing framework after entry

---

## 5.4 Condition B — Bollinger Band interaction

The stock should:
- Touch or pierce the **lower Bollinger Band**
- Then begin reverting upward

### Meaning
This indicates:
- Temporary oversold pressure
- A possible mean-reversion move
- A chance that weakness is exhausting

The idea is not to buy weakness blindly.  
The weakness must be followed by confirmation.

---

## 5.5 Condition C — Green candle

The final trigger is a **bullish daily candle**.

This green candle should ideally:
- Close strongly
- Show reversal strength
- Reclaim or hold important structure
- Move back above the 50 SMA and/or back inside the Bollinger range

This is the actual confirmation event.

---

## 5.6 Entry rule

The instructor uses the same entry discipline seen in earlier strategy modules:

- **Enter at +1% above the trigger candle**

### Why this matters
This avoids:
- Premature entries
- Weak or fake reversals
- Buying a candle that looked good but fails immediately

This rule forces the trader to wait for follow-through.

---

## 5.7 Stop-loss rule

- **Stop-loss = recent daily swing low**

This is straightforward structural risk control.

### Why this makes sense
If price breaks the recent swing low:
- The reversal idea is likely invalid
- The setup has failed
- The trade should be exited

---

## 5.8 Trailing rule

The instructor says to use the:

- **50 SMA as the trailing mechanism**

### Exit logic
As long as price remains above the 50 SMA:
- Continue holding

If price closes decisively below it:
- Exit the trade

This gives a rule-based exit method rather than emotional profit-taking.

---

## 5.9 Additional strategy filters

The instructor makes it clear that the ABC setup should not be traded in isolation.

### Additional context required
- Nifty should not be hostile
- Broader market backdrop should support the trade
- FII flow should not strongly contradict the setup
- Liquidity should be acceptable

So the strategy is not:
> “See green candle, buy.”

It is:
> “See green candle only after broader context, structural setup, and confirmation align.”

# 6. Return Framing and Annualisation Logic

The instructor then discusses trade duration and annualised return framing.

This part is important because it shows how swing traders think about speed of capital rotation.

---

## 6.1 Example 1 — 6.31 days

The instructor gives an example where a move resolves in roughly:

- **6.31 days**

Then frames this as:

- `365 / 6.31 ≈ 57`

Meaning:
- In purely theoretical annual turnover terms, capital could rotate many times in a year

---

## 6.2 Example 2 — 130 days

A second example is given where the trade resolves in about:

- **130 days**

Then framed as:

- `365 / 130 ≈ 2.8`

Meaning:
- The same type of return, if achieved over a much longer holding period, has much lower annualised efficiency

---

## 6.3 Why this is being taught

The instructor compares this mindset against the benchmark of a bank FD, roughly around:

- **7% annual return**

### Core lesson
The edge in swing trading is not just:
- How much you make

It is also:
- How fast the move resolves

### Important caution
This is a framing method, not a promise.

The point is:
- Faster valid setups increase capital efficiency
- Time matters in evaluating a trade
