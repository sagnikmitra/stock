## 5. Strategy 1 — Buying in the Dips

This is presented as the first formal swing-trading strategy of the class.

### Strategic idea
The idea is simple:
- first identify stocks that are already strong on a higher timeframe,
- then wait for a temporary pullback on the daily chart,
- then buy when that pullback begins to resolve upward.

This is not bottom-fishing in weak stocks. It is **buying retracements inside strength**.

### Step-by-step logic

#### Step 1: Monthly RSI filter
The first filter is:
- **Monthly RSI greater than or equal to 60**

This is used to identify stocks that already have strong momentum and are likely in a broader uptrend.

#### Step 2: Shift from monthly to daily
Once a stock satisfies the monthly RSI condition, the trader drops to the **daily chart**.

#### Step 3: Wait for a daily dip
On the daily chart, the trader does not chase price blindly. Instead, they wait for:
- a visible pullback,
- a dip inside the existing uptrend,
- temporary weakness that may offer a more favorable entry.

#### Step 4: Entry point
Entry is taken when the daily dip starts resolving upward.

The wording in the source summary suggests this is based on the daily candle where the stock begins to turn back up after the pullback. In other words, the trader is looking for **resumption of the uptrend after a controlled retracement**.

#### Step 5: Stop-loss
The stop-loss is placed at the recent **daily swing low**.

This is important because it grounds risk in actual chart structure rather than arbitrary percentage logic.

#### Step 6: Management and trailing
The trader is expected to:
- tolerate normal day-to-day noise,
- avoid panicking on small fluctuations,
- trail the position once cleaner higher-low structures begin to form.

### Key principle behind the strategy
The instructor frames the idea using a compact formula:

> price = trend + momentum

In that framing:
- the **monthly RSI ≥ 60** confirms strong trend/momentum context,
- the **daily dip** provides the tactical entry.

## 6. Market Principle — Bulls Are Slow, Bears Are Fast

This is one of the central behavioral principles taught in the session.

### Meaning
- **Bullish moves generally develop gradually**
- **Bearish moves often unfold much faster**

That asymmetry affects trade management.

### Practical implication
For swing traders, this means:
- let valid long trades breathe,
- do not expect uptrends to explode instantly every time,
- but cut bad trades quickly because downside can accelerate faster than upside builds.

This principle supports the structure of the swing systems being taught:
- trend-following longs,
- defined stop-losses,
- patience with good setups,
- fast respect for invalidation.

## 7. Market Principle — Stay with Nifty

Another major rule in the session is the instruction to align swing trades with the direction of **Nifty**.

### Instructor’s framing
The session describes Nifty as the market leader or the “king of the jungle,” with the explanation that a relatively small set of top constituents accounts for a large part of market capitalization.

The practical lesson is straightforward:
- do not swing-trade against the broad market trend,
- use Nifty as directional context,
- treat market alignment as a tailwind.

### Operational rule
Before taking a swing trade, the student should check **Nifty’s daily trend**.

- If Nifty is trending upward, long setups are acceptable.
- If Nifty is weakening or rolling over, the trader should either stay defensive or avoid aggressive long entries.

This is not presented as a highly mathematical filter in the summary. It is positioned more as a **discipline rule** or directional sanity check.

### Why it matters
The instructor uses a tailwind/headwind analogy:
- trading with Nifty is like moving with the wind,
- trading against Nifty is like flying into resistance.

In plain terms, even a decent stock setup can become harder to execute when the broader market is working against it. fileciteturn0file0

## 8. Divergence Concept — RSI and Price Disagreement

The session then introduces **divergence**, specifically in relation to RSI.

### Definition
Divergence means **price and RSI are no longer moving in agreement**.

That disagreement can suggest that the current move is weakening and may reverse.

### Bullish divergence
Bullish divergence occurs when:
- **price makes a lower low**, but
- **RSI makes a higher low**

Interpretation:
- price is still appearing weak on the surface,
- but momentum is no longer weakening at the same pace,
- so the down move may be losing force,
- and an upside reversal becomes more plausible.

### Bearish divergence
Bearish divergence occurs when:
- **price makes a higher high**, but
- **RSI makes a lower high**

Interpretation:
- price is still pushing upward,
- but momentum is fading,
- so the uptrend may be weakening,
- and the probability of downward reversal rises.

### How it is meant to be used
The instructor does not teach divergence here as a standalone mechanical system.
Instead, divergence is meant to act as a **confluence signal**.

That means:
- it strengthens a broader setup,
- it adds context,
- it helps confirm weakening or strengthening momentum,
- but it should not be treated as the only reason to take a trade.

### Tooling note
The session references the **RSI Divergence Indicator** on TradingView as a useful helper, especially for scanning or visual support.

However, the source summary also stresses that students should be capable of reading divergence manually by drawing lines on:
- price structure,
- RSI structure.

That is important, because relying blindly on indicators without understanding the underlying chart relationship is exactly the kind of laziness that eventually causes bad decisions. fileciteturn0file0

## 9. Strategy 2 — Cross Strategy

The second major swing strategy introduced in this class is called the **Cross Strategy**.

This strategy is read on the **daily chart** and uses multiple technical conditions together.

### Strategic idea
The setup is looking for a stock that:
- has pulled into weakness,
- is beginning to reverse,
- is crossing key technical markers,
- and is showing evidence of renewed upward intent.

### Required conditions
According to the source summary, the strategy requires the following daily-chart conditions:

#### 1. Lower Bollinger Band touch
Price should come down and **touch the lower Bollinger Band**.

This indicates short-term weakness or a pullback zone.

#### 2. Trendline break
A downtrend line drawn across recent lower highs should be **broken upward by price**.

This implies that the short-term down move is losing control.

#### 3. VWAP cross
Price should also move **above VWAP**.

This adds another layer of confirmation that price is regaining strength.

#### 4. Green daily candle
A confirming **green candle** is expected as part of the reversal structure.

### Why it is called “Cross Strategy”
The name comes from the combination of crossing events:
- trendline cross,
- VWAP cross,
- reversal structure near the lower Bollinger Band.

The summary refers to the combined setup area as a “cross zone.”

### Entry rule
The entry is not taken exactly at raw trigger level.
Instead, the strategy uses:
- **entry at daily close above the cross zone**, with
- an additional **1% buffer above trigger**.

That 1% buffer is important because it attempts to reduce false triggers and premature entries.

### Stop-loss rule
The stop-loss is set at the recent **daily swing low**.

Again, this is consistent with structure-based risk logic rather than random stop placement.

### Primary trend precondition
This is a critical rule and many traders would ignore it if left vague, so it deserves emphasis.

The strategy is **not valid in a primary downtrend**.

Before taking the trade, the trader should confirm that the broader stock structure is not bearish. The summary notes that the lower Bollinger Band itself should be:
- rising, or
- at least sideways,
- but not falling in a clearly bearish way.

That means the Cross Strategy is intended for:
- reversal within strength,
- or reversal within stability,
- not blind knife-catching in a deteriorating stock.

### Example cited
The source summary specifically mentions **ONGC** as an on-screen example of the strategy, described as a sideways stock beginning to turn upward with a clean cross setup. fileciteturn0file0

## 10. Scanner-Based Workflow

One of the more practically useful points in the session is the insistence on using scanners.

### Why scanners matter
The instructor makes the obvious but necessary point:
- you cannot manually evaluate every possible stock every day,
- especially not across thousands of names,
- and especially not when a strategy depends on multiple technical conditions.

So each swing strategy is associated with a **scanner condition set**.

### Use case
Scanners are meant to:
- narrow the stock universe,
- surface candidates meeting the defined strategy conditions,
- reduce random stock selection,
- improve discipline.

### Discipline rule
A major rule repeated in the summary is:

> Do not enter a stock that the scanner did not surface.

That is a strong process rule. The point is to prevent students from improvising setups out of boredom, bias, or greed.

### Practical distinction
The scanner is presented purely as a **selection tool**.
It is not the broker, not the execution engine, and not the account platform itself.

So if a student has broker-related issues, blocked access, or unrelated platform problems, those do not change the logic of the trading setup. fileciteturn0file0

## 11. Discipline, Psychology, and Decision-Making

A large part of the session reinforces a recurring teaching stance:

> The market is mostly psychology and discipline, not just technical strategy.

### The 80/20 framing
The instructor reportedly says:
- **80% of the stock market is psychological**
- **20% is strategy**

Whether one agrees with the exact ratio is secondary. The message is clear:
- a workable strategy alone is not enough,
- execution consistency matters more,
- emotional instability ruins even decent systems.

### Tailwinds vs headwinds
The session uses an aircraft analogy:
- moving with the wind helps speed and efficiency,
- moving against the wind increases resistance and effort.

Applied to trading:
- align with Nifty for tailwind,
- avoid fighting the broader market.

### No strategy is perfect
The class also emphasizes that no strategy will be right every single time.

That matters because beginners often destroy themselves with one of two mistakes:
- they abandon a decent system after a few losses,
- or they overcomplicate the system trying to eliminate all losses.

The session pushes the healthier view:
- losses are part of the framework,
- the objective is not perfection,
- the objective is a process where wins meaningfully outweigh losses over time.

### Focus and routine
The summary notes repetition of a mindset line:

> Where your focus goes, your energy flows.

This is basically a reminder that:
- scattered attention leads to impulsive behavior,
- consistency matters,
- traders need routine, record-keeping, observation, and back-testing rather than random excitement.

### Avoid indicator overload
A student question about mixing indicators leads to a useful caution:
- adding too many indicators creates paralysis,
- complexity can look intelligent but often makes execution worse,
- a few well-understood signals are better than a noisy pile of technical clutter. fileciteturn0file0
