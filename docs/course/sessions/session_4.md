## 6.2 Support and Resistance

The first major technical block is support and resistance.

### Support

Support is described as the area where buying interest becomes strong enough to absorb or overpower selling pressure. In practical terms, it is the area where price has historically found buyers and either paused or reversed upward.

### Resistance

Resistance is described as the opposite condition: an area where selling pressure becomes strong enough to absorb or overpower buying interest. In practice, price often stalls, rejects, or reverses downward from such an area.

### Important teaching nuance

The instructor emphasizes that support and resistance are **not exact numbers** in a rigid sense. They should be treated as:

- zones,
- reaction areas,
- clusters of price memory,

rather than as perfect, single-tick lines.

This is important because beginners often make a major mistake: they draw one exact line and then assume that a one-tick breach invalidates the concept. The teaching here rejects that oversimplification.

### Structural principle

The instructor also emphasizes the common role-flip behavior:

- old resistance can become new support after breakout,
- old support can become new resistance after breakdown.

That role reversal is a core market-structure idea and forms part of the conceptual foundation for later zone-based thinking.

### Student takeaway

A good student note here would be:

> Do not obsess over exact price precision. Focus on reaction zones and repeated behavioral areas.

## 6.3 Order Blocks and Reversal Interpretation

After introducing classical support and resistance, the session expands into order-block style interpretation.

### What the session suggests an order block is

The instructor frames an order block as a specific candle or small cluster of candles around a reversal context where larger market participation appears to have been absorbed before price moves in the opposite direction.

This explanation is not presented as a formal institutional-definition lecture. It is presented more as a practical trader’s reading framework.

### Bullish reversal checklist described in the session

A simplified order-block-style bullish reversal read is described as something like:

1. price falls into a meaningful level,
2. selling pressure starts weakening,
3. volume dries up or loses conviction,
4. an order-block-style candle appears,
5. price stalls,
6. reversal confirmation appears.

### Timeframe discipline

A major warning accompanies this section:

- do not chase every intraday fluctuation,
- do not treat every candle as a signal,
- confirm properly,
- prefer daily-candle observation for reliability.

That warning matters because the instructor is clearly trying to prevent overtrading and signal addiction.

### Embedded behavioral rule

The underlying behavioral principle is:

> Entry must happen only when pre-defined conditions are present.

The instructor reinforces this using a personal analogy about not “firing” unless the conditions are right. The point is straightforward: execution should be conditional, not reactive.

## 6.7 Supply and Demand Zones

This is the core technical centerpiece of the second half of the session.

The instructor presents supply and demand zones as a richer framework than single-line support and resistance.

### Zone versus line

The distinction is important:

- support/resistance is often marked as a line,
- supply/demand is treated as a zone or range.

A zone is usually drawn around a base or area of consolidation from which price later makes a strong move.

This is a more flexible and structurally realistic way to think about price behavior.

### Four canonical zone structures introduced in the session

#### 1. DBR — Drop, Base, Rally

- Price drops,
- forms a base or consolidation area,
- then rallies strongly upward.

This is presented as a **demand-zone structure**.

#### 2. RBR — Rally, Base, Rally

- Price rallies,
- pauses or bases,
- then rallies again.

This is also treated as a **demand-zone structure**, typically within a broader uptrend continuation context.

#### 3. RBD — Rally, Base, Drop

- Price rallies,
- forms a base,
- then drops.

This is presented as a **supply-zone structure**.

#### 4. DBD — Drop, Base, Drop

- Price drops,
- forms a base,
- then continues dropping.

This is also treated as a **supply-zone structure**, often inside a broader downtrend continuation context.

### Important interpretation rule

The instructor reportedly stresses that there is **no single magical invalidation price** inside this framework. Small wick penetrations into the zone do not automatically mean the zone has failed.

Instead, a decisive break is interpreted more structurally, such as:

- stronger close beyond the zone,
- real structural failure,
- clearer evidence that the zone is no longer holding.

### Why this matters for beginners

This section is critical because it teaches students to stop thinking in ultra-binary terms. Market structure is often messy. Zones are lived areas of conflict, not perfectly clean textbook rectangles.

### Practical student note

> Mark zones as areas of origin for strong price moves, not as razor-thin lines.

## 7. Core Concepts Extracted from the Session

This section normalizes the key concepts into Claude Code–friendly reference form.

### 7.1 Concept: Support

**Definition**: An area where buying interest historically becomes strong enough to slow, stop, or reverse a decline.

**Practical interpretation**:

- derived from prior lows,
- should be treated as a zone,
- useful for observing possible buyer activity.

### 7.2 Concept: Resistance

**Definition**: An area where selling interest historically becomes strong enough to slow, stop, or reverse an advance.

**Practical interpretation**:

- derived from prior highs,
- should be treated as a zone,
- useful for observing possible seller activity.

### 7.3 Concept: Role Reversal

**Definition**: A previously important resistance area may later behave as support after a breakout, and vice versa.

### 7.4 Concept: Order Block (session-level working interpretation)

**Definition**: A reversal-linked candle or candle cluster marking a possible area of meaningful order absorption before price moves the other way.

**Session usage**:

- practical and observational,
- not deeply formalized,
- used to improve reversal reading.

### 7.5 Concept: Liquidity Hunt

**Definition**: Price moving into obvious stop areas before reversing, often catching traders who place stops at predictable levels.

### 7.6 Concept: Supply Zone

**Definition**: A range from which price previously departed strongly downward.

**Typical patterns**:

- RBD,
- DBD.

### 7.7 Concept: Demand Zone

**Definition**: A range from which price previously departed strongly upward.

**Typical patterns**:

- DBR,
- RBR.
