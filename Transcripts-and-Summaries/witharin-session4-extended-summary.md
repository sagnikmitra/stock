## Teaching arc

### 1. Recap and framing

The session opens with a short recap of the previous three classes — history of the market, chart patterns, candlestick patterns, and a simple SMA-20 strategy. The instructor tells students that today they move from “what the price looks like” to “where the price reacts”, i.e. from patterns to levels.

### 2. Support and resistance

The first major block of teaching is support and resistance.

- **Support** is defined as the price level where buying interest is strong enough to overcome selling pressure — the area where the market has historically found buyers and stopped falling.
- **Resistance** is the mirror: a level where selling interest is strong enough to overcome buying pressure, and price has historically stalled or reversed down.
- Support is identified from previous lows; resistance from previous highs. The instructor emphasises that these are drawn as **levels/zones**, not a single perfect price, and that the same level can flip role (old resistance becomes new support after a breakout).
- A recurring point: don’t obsess over one exact tick — think in terms of reaction areas.

### 3. Order blocks and reversal reads

The class then extends beyond classical S/R into order-block style reads.

- An order block is introduced as a specific reversal-cluster candle that marks where large orders were absorbed, typically followed by a strong move in the opposite direction.
- The instructor describes a simple mental checklist for a bullish reversal setup: falling candles into a level, volume drying up, an order-block candle, price stalling, then a reversal candle.
- Strong emphasis on patience: order blocks must be **observed on daily candles**, confirmed, and not chased intraday on every small signal.
- He ties this back to a broader rule: do not enter unless the setup matches the plan. He uses a personal analogy — “I fire only when the conditions are right, otherwise I don’t fire” — to frame trade entry as conditional, not reflexive.

### 4. Patience, discipline and the wildlife-photographer analogy

A long motivational block in the middle of the class uses Indian wildlife photographer **Dhritiman Mukherjee** as a metaphor for trading discipline:

- He spends enormous time waiting for a single decisive moment (a leopard in active motion, etc.) before pressing the shutter.
- Trading, the instructor argues, is the same: you wait for your specific condition, you do not shoot at everything that moves, and you accept that most of your time is spent observing, not acting.
- He extends this into self-discipline: the instructor says he has built a habit of never being late to class, and uses that as an example of the compounding value of small, consistent rules. Patience + discipline are repeatedly framed as the real edge, not any single indicator.

### 5. Risk, constraints, and realistic capital expectations

The class pivots to risk and constraints:

- Students are reminded that they are retail traders with **limited capital**, and must trade within those limits. The running example is roughly: if your working capital is small, you cannot aim for outsized absolute returns — you size to a realistic percentage per trade (the discussion references rough ~5% style sizing ideas, not as a hard rule but as a sanity anchor).
- Key message: know your constraints before you design a strategy. A plan that is valid for someone with large capital is not automatically valid for you.
- This is also where the class introduces **retail context, stop-loss liquidity, and pending-area concepts** — price levels where stops pile up and where price tends to hunt liquidity before reversing. Students are told to factor these into where they put stops, not just where they enter.

### 6. Supply and demand zones

The technical centrepiece of the second half is **supply and demand zones**, positioned as an upgrade from single-line support/resistance.

- The instructor distinguishes zones from lines: support/resistance is a single price; supply/demand is a **range** drawn around a base where price consolidated before a strong move.
- He lays out the four canonical base patterns:
  - **DBR – Drop, Base, Rally** (demand zone formed after a drop and consolidation, followed by a rally up).
  - **RBR – Rally, Base, Rally** (demand zone inside an existing uptrend).
  - **RBD – Rally, Base, Drop** (supply zone formed after a rally and consolidation, followed by a drop).
  - **DBD – Drop, Base, Drop** (supply zone inside an existing downtrend).
- He stresses that in the supply/demand framework there is **no exact violation price** — you are working with a zone, so small wicks through it are expected. The zone is broken only on a decisive close / structural break.
- Pattern-for-demand vs pattern-for-supply are introduced as the labels students should use when marking charts.

### 7. Tooling and indicator discipline

Toward the late portion, the instructor talks about indicators and platforms:

- He recommends **TradingView** as the primary charting platform for the cohort and points students toward its built-in indicators rather than hunting exotic tools.
- Message: learn a small number of indicators deeply. Over-indicator-ing is a common beginner trap. Any setup should still be readable without stacking five tools on top of it.

The session ends on an explicit expectation-setter:

- The instructor tells students that sessions up to this point are effectively a **warm-up period**. The “actual game” — the part where they should expect to act on setups in the live market — is framed as starting around session 19.
- The analogy: you do not jump into a pool and immediately swim fast; you warm up, you build technique, you build discipline, and only then you compete.

## Main takeaways a student should write down

1. Session 4 is the bridge from pattern recognition to **level- and zone-based** reads of the market.
2. Support/resistance are **zones, not exact prices**; old resistance becomes new support.
3. Order blocks extend the idea — look for: falling candles → volume dry → order-block candle → stall → reversal candle, confirmed on daily timeframe.
4. Supply/demand framework uses four base structures: **DBR, RBR, RBD, DBD**. Demand from DBR/RBR, supply from RBD/DBD. Zones have no single violation price.
5. Trading is mostly waiting. The wildlife-photographer analogy (Dhritiman Mukherjee) is the instructor’s canonical metaphor for patience and discipline.
6. Risk must be sized to your real capital and constraints; stop-loss placement should account for liquidity hunts around obvious levels.
7. Use TradingView + a small, well-understood indicator set. Do not stack tools.
8. The foundational sessions are a warm-up. The real live-market push is later in the course.
